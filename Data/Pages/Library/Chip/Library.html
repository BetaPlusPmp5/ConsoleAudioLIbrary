Sony SPC700 (SNES)

The sound chip that brought 16-bit music to life.

The Sony SPC700 was the dedicated audio processor of the Super Nintendo Entertainment System (SNES). Unlike earlier systems where sound shared resources with the CPU, the SPC700 operated independently with its own RAM and digital signal processor (DSP), delivering high-quality, sample-based audio that redefined game music.

With 64 KB of audio RAM and support for 8 simultaneous channels of compressed PCM samples, the SPC700 gave composers far more freedom than 8-bit systems. From sweeping orchestral scores to crunchy voice samples, it enabled developers to push creative boundaries and craft iconic soundtracks still celebrated today.

Ricoh 2A03 (NES)

The chip that made 8-bit music legendary.

The Ricoh 2A03 is more than just a CPU. It contains the entire NES audio processing unit, giving composers five distinct sound channels and forcing them to innovate within tight technical boundaries. The two square wave channels, triangle bass, noise generator, and one-bit sample playback defined an era of music.

This chip, though limited by today's standards, taught developers and musicians alike how to make the most out of every byte. With no volume control on the triangle wave and a single sample channel that slowed down the system, it was both a challenge and a creative engine.
